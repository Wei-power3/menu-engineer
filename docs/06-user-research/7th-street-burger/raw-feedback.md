# Raw Feedback — 7th Street Burger

> Verbatim quotes preserved unedited. For synthesis and analysis see `report.md`.

**Date:** 6 April 2026
**Menu tested:** 8 items — Cheeseburger $6.50, Double $9.50, Impossible Veg $8.00, Double Veg $11.50, Fries $4.50, Water $2.00, Mexican Coke $4.00, Sauce $1.00
**Settings:** Fast Casual / QSR, 30% / 27% food cost benchmark pre-fill, US market

---

## Respondent 1 — Owner (26 locations, PE-backed)

### What works well

> "The scan-to-populate feature is genuinely useful. Pulling 8 items off a menu photo in seconds is the kind of time-saving shortcut an operator like me actually values. I'm not sitting at a laptop — I'm on the floor. The faster the setup, the better."

> "The Kasavana & Smith matrix (Stars / Puzzles / Plowhorses / Dogs) is a solid, established framework. It's been around since 1982 for a reason. Presenting it in a clean visual format makes it accessible without needing to know the academic background. That's good design."

> "The Price Adjustment Map table is clean and scannable. I can look at it in 10 seconds and know what's happening across the whole menu. That's how I like it — no fluff."

### What concerns me

> "The tool itself flags the biggest problem: it's estimating popularity from price position, not actual sales data. That's a significant caveat, and to their credit they acknowledge it upfront. But for me, this essentially makes the 'Puzzle' vs. 'Plowhorse' split unreliable. My Cheeseburger at $6.50 is probably my highest-volume item — the whole brand is built on it. The tool is calling it a 'Puzzle' (high margin, low popularity) because its price is above the menu average of $5.81. That's backwards from reality."

> "The 'Raise price' recommendation on Water ($2.00) and Sauce ($1.00) also gives me pause. Raising water by $1–2 would push it to $3–4 — that's a brand signal. We built 7th Street on value and accessibility. I'm not raising water to $4. These are judgment calls that require knowing the brand, not just the numbers."

> "All four burgers getting the same 'Hold price / Move it higher on the menu' recommendation feels templated. What does 'move it higher' mean when you're operating a digital menu board and a third-party delivery app? That advice needs to be context-specific for the format."

### Verdict

> "For a solo operator setting up their first menu, or someone who's never heard of menu engineering, this tool is genuinely valuable. It introduces the right framework, it's fast, and the output is readable. The PDF export option is a nice touch for sharing with a co-founder or an investor."

> "For a scaled operator like me — someone with 26 locations, a PE backer, and POS data sitting in Toast — I'd outgrow this within one use. The moment you have real sales volume, you need to plug that in. The tool acknowledges this (says volume input is 'planned for a future update'), and honestly that's the single feature that would make this a serious operations tool rather than a good starting point."

> **Score: 7/10** — "Solid for early-stage. Needs actual volume data input to be trustworthy for anyone running a real chain. Build that, and I'd actually use it."

## Respondent 2 — General Manager (same chain)

### First impression

> "Clean. Fast. No fluff."

> "The scan worked — it pulled all 8 items correctly. That's already saving me time vs. manually typing everything in. The one-click 'Analyse 8 items' button is clear. No confusion about what to do next."

### What works well

> "The Price Adjustment Map table is the most immediately useful thing. It's sortable, shows margin ranked low to high, and the 'Raise / Hold' recommendation is direct. I can screenshot this and bring it to a conversation with my regional manager without needing to explain methodology."

> "The logic is transparent. It shows why something is categorized the way it is — e.g., 'Margin $3.28 is below menu avg $4.24 → low margin. Price $4.50 is below or equal to menu avg $5.81 → estimated high popularity.' That's not just a label — that's something I can defend to HQ."

> "Fast food cost prefill. Pre-filling 27% food cost for QSR/Fast Food is a good default. I didn't have to look anything up."

### Where it falls short

> "The popularity model is guesswork. The tool flags this itself — 'Popularity is estimated from price position, not actual sales data.' This is a real limitation. At 7th Street Burger, the Double and the Cheeseburger are probably our top sellers. Calling them 'Puzzles' with 'low visibility' because they're above the average price could be misleading. Without actual order count data from our POS, this is directional at best."

> "No Stars. That zero in the Stars column is the most important number on the page, and the tool doesn't comment on it at all. It should probably say something like: 'You have no Stars — this means no item is both high-margin and high-volume. This is either a pricing opportunity or a data gap.' That would prompt real action."

> "The advice is the same for every Puzzle and every Plowhorse. 'Move it higher on the menu or add a compelling description' — that's the exact same sentence for all four burger items. Same formula copy-pasted. For something like the SAUCE at $1.00, 'raise by $1–2' means tripling the margin but also potentially making it a friction point at checkout. Context-sensitive advice would be more useful."

> "No 'Dogs' section guidance. Zero Dogs is actually good news, but the tool just says 'No items in this category.' A quick note like 'Clean menu — no low-margin, low-popularity items to cut' would close the loop."

> "No upsell / bundle angle. In fast-casual, the real margin play is often combo logic — Burger + Fries + Drink. This tool looks at items in isolation. If Fries and Mexican Coke are both Plowhorses individually, bundling them with a high-margin burger at a slight discount could shift the whole picture. That's missing."

### Verdict

> "Would I use this? Yes, for a first pass — especially before a review with my regional manager. The scan feature, the matrix, and the sortable price table are genuinely useful. It gets me to a structured conversation faster than a spreadsheet would."

> "Would I trust it alone? No. The popularity estimation based on price position is too blunt for a real operational decision, especially for a tight menu like ours where every item has a clear role. I'd want to layer in actual sales data before acting on any of these recommendations."

> **Verdict: Solid starter tool. Useful for prompting the right questions. Not a replacement for knowing your own numbers.**
